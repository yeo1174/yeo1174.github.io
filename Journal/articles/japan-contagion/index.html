The Next Domino: How Japan's Yen Crisis Could Become America's Problem

With the yen at 40-year lows and Tokyo running out of options, a destabilized Japan could trigger a sell-off in U.S. Treasuries that tests the resilience of American financial markets.
By AL
TOKYO — The dollar fetched 162 yen Tuesday, a level not seen since 1986. For most investors, it is just another data point in a long-running currency trend. For a growing number of economists and hedge fund managers, it is the canary in an increasingly dark coal mine.

The Japanese yen has lost more than half its value against the U.S. dollar over the past five years, a collapse without modern precedent for a major advanced economy. And for the first time in the post-war era, that collapse is raising a question that would have seemed absurd a decade ago: Could Japan's currency crisis trigger a financial crisis — in the United States?

The conventional wisdom holds that Japan’s problems stay in Japan. With 95% of its massive government debt held domestically, the country lacks the vulnerability to foreign capital flight that sank Greece or Argentina. But that comforting narrative ignores a critical fact: Japanese institutions are among the largest holders of U.S. financial assets in the world. If Japan faces a domestic financial crunch, the selling pressure could cascade directly into America’s most important market.

“Japan is the world’s largest creditor nation with a collapsing currency. That contradiction cannot resolve itself peacefully.” — Nomura Research Institute, June 2026

The Trap

Bank of Japan Governor Kazuo Ueda faces a dilemma that admits no good outcome. Japan’s benchmark interest rate stands at 1%, raised in a widely expected 25-basis-point move on June 16. The Federal Reserve’s rate is above 5%. That 400-basis-point gap makes the yen a funding currency of choice for traders worldwide, who borrow cheaply in yen and invest in higher-yielding dollar assets. The carry trade, estimated by some analysts at $1 trillion to $2 trillion in notional value, is a relentless structural force pushing the yen lower.

“The BOJ can raise rates, but it cannot raise them to 5% without breaking its own economy,” said Julia Wang, chief investment officer for North Asia at Nomura. “Intervention is a stopgap, not a solution. The market understands this.”

Japan spent more than $70 billion defending the yen in May. The effect lasted roughly three weeks. The yen then resumed its slide, breaching 162 on Tuesday and prompting Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama to reiterate that Tokyo is “ready to take decisive action.”

The math behind the BOJ’s paralysis is brutal. Japan’s government debt stands at 250% of gross domestic product, the highest in the developed world. Every percentage-point increase in interest rates adds roughly 2.5% of GDP to annual debt-servicing costs. A rate path that could meaningfully defend the yen would require JGB yields that Japan’s banking system cannot withstand.

The Contagion Mechanism

The transmission belt from Tokyo to Wall Street runs through a single $27 trillion market: U.S. Treasuries. Japan is the largest foreign holder of American government debt, with approximately $1.1 trillion in Treasury securities held by Japanese banks, pension funds, and insurance companies.

Here is the mechanism that keeps central bankers awake at night. If Japan’s economy deteriorates further — if the yen falls to 170, if import inflation forces the BOJ into emergency action, if regional banks begin to fail — Japanese institutions would face a liquidity or solvency crisis that demands repatriation of capital. Those institutions hold massive dollar-denominated portfolios. Forced selling of U.S. Treasuries would be the natural response.

“The market is underappreciating the tail risk,” said a senior trader at a Japanese megabank who declined to be named discussing internal risk assessments. “If even one major life insurer needs to liquidate its dollar holdings in a disorderly way, everyone looks at the door at the same time.”

Japan has been here before — barely. During the Global Financial Crisis of 2008, Japanese banks repatriated capital from dollar assets, contributing to the strain on U.S. money markets. The Fed responded with swap lines that effectively guaranteed dollar liquidity for the BOJ. Those swap lines remain active today. But the question is whether they can manage the orderly unwind of a carry trade that has grown an order of magnitude larger over the intervening 18 years.

Why This Time Could Be Different

Three structural factors make the current situation more dangerous than past Japan-related scares.

First, the scale of the carry trade. Zero interest rates in Japan from 2016 to 2024 encouraged an unprecedented build-up of yen-funded positions in global markets. Analysts at the Bank for International Settlements estimate that cross-border yen-denominated lending has grown to roughly $3 trillion. A disorderly unwinding of these positions would dwarf the 1998 Long-Term Capital Management crisis or the 2008 repo market dislocation.

Second, the U.S. market is itself stretched. As investor Jeremy Grantham has warned, American equities trade at 35-40 times cyclically adjusted earnings, among the highest valuations in history. The S&P 500’s top-10 concentration exceeds the dot-com peak. The U.S. fiscal deficit stands at $1.9 trillion for fiscal year 2026 alone, with the Congressional Budget Office projecting debt reaching 120% of GDP by 2036. America has less room to absorb an externally generated shock than it did in previous cycles.

Third, the geopolitical safety net is fraying. France repatriated its gold from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York between July 2025 and January 2026. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney announced a $25 billion sovereign wealth fund designed to reduce dependence on the United States. German officials have publicly questioned the wisdom of storing 37% of the country’s gold reserves in New York. The dollar’s share of global foreign exchange reserves has fallen from 71% in 1999 to 57% today. Allies, not just adversaries, are quietly reducing exposure to the dollar-based system. Cooperation on a joint stabilization effort can no longer be taken for granted.

“These are not the actions of enemies. They are the actions of allies who have quietly concluded they need to reduce their exposure.” — Sana Ur Rehman, EBC Financial Group, on de-dollarization by U.S. allies

The Bear Case Against the Bear Case

Counterpoint: For every bear scenario, there are seasoned investors who argue that Japan’s crisis remains contained. The BOJ can always buy unlimited JGBs to cap yields, preventing the banking system stress that would force asset sales. The Fed maintains swap lines with the BOJ that can provide dollar liquidity if Japanese institutions face a cash crunch. And a sudden yen strengthening — paradoxically triggered by the very crisis that bears predict — could rapidly unwind the carry-trade pressure that has driven the currency to 40-year lows. “Japan is broke on paper but flush in practice,” said one veteran Tokyo-based analyst. “The system adapts. It always has.”

A Parallel to the Unthinkable

Former Treasury Secretary Lawrence Summers wrote in 2024 that the pricing of U.S. sovereign risk “remains inexplicably serene relative to the trajectory of debt and deficits.” His observation has only gained force since. The Congressional Budget Office projects the deficit will rise from $1.9 trillion this year to $3.1 trillion by 2036. By that year, federal debt held by the public is projected to reach 120% of GDP — surpassing the record set in the aftermath of World War II.

In this environment, the idea that America’s largest foreign creditor could become a forced seller of Treasuries is not merely a technical risk. It is a stress test for the entire architecture of the post-war financial system. If Japanese institutions were to begin liquidating their dollar holdings in earnest, the Federal Reserve would face an agonizing choice between protecting the Treasury market and defending the dollar’s purchasing power.

The Precedent That Haunts

The last time a major allied economy’s financial distress spread to the United States was 1998, when Russia’s default and the collapse of Long-Term Capital Management froze global credit markets. The Fed, then under Alan Greenspan, orchestrated a bailout of LTCM by its creditors and cut rates three times in rapid succession.

Today’s version would be Japan’s version. The BOJ’s G7 partners would be forced to decide whether a coordinated intervention — the first since the 2011 earthquake response — is necessary to prevent a broader contagion. But in 1998, the U.S. was a creditor nation with a budget surplus, and the Fed had ammunition. In 2026, the Fed is operating with inflation above target and a fiscal deficit that leaves limited room for expansionary policy.

“The playbook from 1998 assumes the U.S. comes in as the strong, stable center of gravity,” said the Japanese megabank trader. “This time, the center of gravity has its own cracks.”

What to Watch

Investors should monitor three indicators that would signal a transition from Japan’s slow-burn crisis to a systemic event:

The yen at 170. Each 5-yen decline raises the political pressure on the BOJ to act. At 170, the carry-trade carry would be irresistible to speculators, and Japan’s import bill would be catastrophic. That level would force a policy response.

JGB yields above 2%. Japan’s 10-year government bond yield has crept toward 1.5% as the BOJ tapers its purchases. A spike above 2% would threaten the capital positions of regional banks holding trillions in JGBs.

U.S. Treasury auction dynamics. If Japanese participation in Treasury auctions noticeably declines, the signal would precede an actual sell-off. The Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee’s quarterly reports and individual auction bid-to-cover ratios would show the first signs.

None of these thresholds has been breached yet. But the yen at 162 is closer to 170 than to 150. The margin for error is shrinking.
