The Everything Bubble: How AI Mania Echoes the Dot-Com Delusion

Jeremy Grantham calls the AI boom "the biggest investment bubble in American history." With the S&P 500 more concentrated than at the dot-com peak and Big Tech on track to spend $700 billion on AI infrastructure this year alone, the parallels to 1999 are becoming impossible to ignore.
By AL
BOSTON — "The probability that AI will not bust are slim to none; it meets every condition of the railroads and the internet."

That was Jeremy Grantham, the 86-year-old co-founder of GMO and one of the most respected bubble spotters in market history, speaking on Bloomberg's Merryn Talks Money podcast in January 2026. He was doing what he has done for four decades: standing in front of a freight train of market euphoria and telling anyone who would listen that it is going to end badly.

Grantham called the Japanese bubble of the 1980s. He called the dot-com bubble of the 1990s. In September 2007, he wrote in Fortune that the U.S. housing market was in “genuine bubble territory” — months before the Global Financial Crisis. Now he is pointing at the same pattern in artificial intelligence. And he is not alone.

The data, viewed dispassionately, suggests he may be right. The question is not whether we are in a bubble — bubbles, after all, are only identifiable with certainty in hindsight. The question is whether this bubble is different from every other one that came before.

The Grantham Thesis: A Bubble Within a Bubble

Grantham’s argument, laid out in his January 2026 paper coauthored with financial historian Edward Chancellor — titled Valuing AI: Extreme Bubble, New Golden Era, or Both? — rests on a layered diagnosis. The U.S. stock market, he contends, was already dangerously overvalued before ChatGPT arrived in November 2022. The original “super-bubble” had been inflating through 2021. The S&P 500 actually fell about 25% from January through October 2022. Then artificial intelligence changed the narrative.

“The day after Chat came out, the Mag Seven lifted the market on its broad shoulders and staggered forward,” Grantham told Fortune in April 2026. The AI boom didn’t fix the underlying problem, he argued — it deferred it while making it larger.

“The day after Chat came out, the Mag Seven lifted the market on its broad shoulders and staggered forward.” — Jeremy Grantham, Fortune interview, April 2026

The numbers support the metaphor. The five largest companies in the S&P 500 now account for roughly 30% of the index’s total market capitalization — the highest concentration in half a century, exceeding even the dot-com peak in 2000. The cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings (CAPE) ratio for the S&P 500 stands at levels exceeded only in 1929, 1972, 1999–2000, and 2021. Each of those prior extremes was followed by devastating corrections.

GMO defines a bubble as a “two-standard-deviation divergence of the price of any asset class above its long-term real price trend.” By that definition, Grantham argues, the U.S. stock market has been in bubble territory for a prolonged period. The AI mania has merely prolonged the inevitable.

Valuation: Then vs. Now

The simplest comparison is the most instructive. The Nasdaq-100 traded at roughly 60 times forward earnings at its peak in March 2000. Today, the S&P 500 trades at approximately 23 times forward earnings — less than half the multiple. This is the bulls’ favorite data point, and it is a legitimate one.

But the difference in valuation multiples is partly explained by a difference in composition. In 2000, the largest companies in the market were speculative enterprises burning cash. Cisco Systems, briefly the most valuable company in the world at a $550 billion market capitalization, traded at 130 times earnings. Pets.com had a market cap of $300 million and negative revenue growth. The vast majority of dot-com companies were unprofitable at the peak — only about 14% had positive earnings.

Today’s AI giants are different creatures. NVIDIA generated $215.9 billion in revenue in fiscal 2026, up 65% year-over-year, and trades at a forward P/E of roughly 47. Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, and Meta are among the most profitable enterprises in human history. Their earnings are real, their cash flows are enormous, and their market dominance is established.

And yet. NVIDIA’s market capitalization of roughly $4.3 trillion makes it the most valuable company on earth — roughly eight times what Cisco was worth at the peak of the dot-com bubble, in inflation-adjusted terms. Its stock has appreciated roughly 1,300% since late 2022. The market is pricing in not just AI’s current promise, but a level of future dominance that would require the technology to transform virtually every sector of the global economy.

The Capital Expenditure Arms Race

Perhaps the most striking parallel to the dot-com era is the scale of capital flowing into AI infrastructure — and the mounting evidence that much of it is being wasted.

Wall Street’s largest banks now project that Big Tech will spend between $650 billion and $700 billion on AI-related capital expenditure in 2026 alone. Amazon alone is on track for roughly $200 billion, Google approximately $175 billion, Microsoft $145 billion, and Meta between $115 and $135 billion. For context, total technology spending by all U.S. companies at the height of the dot-com boom was roughly $120 billion per year.

Goldman Sachs projects cumulative AI capex of $7.6 trillion between 2026 and 2031. Bain & Company estimates that by 2030, AI companies will need $2 trillion of annual revenue just to meet worldwide compute needs — a figure far beyond anything the industry currently generates.

And the returns so far have been elusive. A comprehensive MIT study published in August 2025 found that despite $30–40 billion in enterprise generative AI investment, 95% of organizations are getting zero return. The gap between spending and monetization is now so wide that even some AI executives have begun to sound the alarm. In late 2024, Databricks CEO Ali Ghodsi called it “peak AI bubble” — this as his own company’s valuation proceeded to more than double, from $62 billion to $134 billion, over the following 15 months.

“The great bubbles always occur around the very most important ideas.” — Jeremy Grantham, Diary of a CEO podcast, June 2026

The Dark Side of Private Markets

The euphoria is not confined to public equities. Private markets are doing things that have no historical precedent.

In February 2026, OpenAI closed a $110 billion funding round — the largest private fundraising in history — at a $730 billion valuation. Amazon contributed $50 billion. NVIDIA and SoftBank each committed $30 billion. Two weeks later, Anthropic raised $30 billion at a $380 billion valuation. These are not public companies. They have no obligation to disclose their financials. And investors are assigning them valuations that would rank them among the 20 largest corporations in the world by market capitalization.

To put that in perspective: OpenAI’s $730 billion valuation is roughly equal to the entire market capitalization of the S&P 500’s energy sector. It is larger than the market caps of Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, and McDonald’s combined.

Venture capital flows into AI reached $258.7 billion globally in 2025, representing 61% of all global VC investment — nearly double the 30% share in 2022. The five largest deals alone accounted for approximately $63 billion. The mania has not gone unnoticed by the fund managers who allocate capital: an October 2025 Bank of America survey found that 54% of global fund managers believe AI stocks are in “bubble territory.” And yet they keep buying.

The Grantham Precedent: How to Be Right and Lose Clients

There is a reason Grantham’s warnings carry weight, and it is not merely that he has been right before. It is that he has been right before, and it cost him.

During the dot-com mania, Grantham and GMO refused to own many technology companies at the valuations the market demanded. The firm lost clients in droves. Most of those clients never came back — even after Grantham was proven right. The experience left him with a cynical but honest assessment of the investment industry’s incentives.

“What we found then is that it’s not a business strategy to be a bear in a bubble,” he said. “You have to be a bull. You can’t take the career risk of the bull market going on a year or two longer than you thought. So you jump off the cliff together. And as Keynes said, if you do that, you will never lose your job. Just be a little quicker and slicker and get out a little faster and get back in more timely.”

The quote is a devastating indictment of an industry in which being wrong conventionally is safer than being right unconventionally. It is also a reminder that even the most accurate bubble-callers cannot force the market to correct on their timeline. Grantham warned about the dot-com bubble in 1998. It took two more years to pop.

“It’s not a business strategy to be a bear in a bubble. You have to be a bull. You can’t take the career risk of the bull market going on a year or two longer than you thought. So you jump off the cliff together.” — Jeremy Grantham, Merryn Talks Money, January 2026

The Structural Differences That Matter

To dismiss the AI boom as a simple replay of 1999 would be intellectually lazy. The structural differences between the two eras are material, and they cut both ways.

Profitability. In 2000, most dot-com companies had no earnings and no clear path to them. Pets.com burned through $300 million in 18 months and collapsed. Webvan spent $1.2 billion building automated warehouses and went bankrupt. Today’s AI leaders — NVIDIA, Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta — generate massive profits. NVIDIA alone earned more in fiscal 2026 than the entire S&P 500 information technology sector earned in 1999. This is a genuine difference, and it provides a floor that the dot-com era lacked.

Adoption. In 1999, roughly 40% of U.S. households had internet access. E-commerce accounted for less than 1% of retail sales. The technology was promising but unproven. In 2026, 71% of organizations regularly use generative AI in at least one business function. OpenAI alone reports 800 million weekly active users. AI is embedded in search, advertising, cloud computing, healthcare, and defense. The technology is real, and it is being deployed at scale.

Debt versus equity. A clear difference with previous bubbles is that the AI capex boom has been largely funded by equity, not debt. The main AI players are in healthy financial condition. When the dot-com bubble burst, hundreds of companies were forced into bankruptcy because they were leveraged. Today’s megacap tech companies can absorb a significant correction without facing insolvency.

The Structural Similarities That Worry

And yet the alarm signals are flashing at frequencies that cannot be dismissed.

Concentration. The top five stocks account for 30% of the S&P 500 — almost double the 16% concentration at the dot-com peak. The market’s fate depends on a handful of names in a way that has not been true since before the Great Depression. If NVIDIA’s earnings growth decelerates, there is no second tier to catch the market.

Capital destruction. The MIT finding that 95% of enterprises are getting zero return on generative AI investment is the 2026 equivalent of the 2000 realization that Pets.com had no business model. The technology may be transformative in the long run, but in the short run, trillions of dollars are being deployed into infrastructure that may not generate an adequate return for years — if ever. The parallels to the fiber-optic overbuild of the late 1990s are uncanny. Between 1996 and 2001, telecom companies laid 40 million miles of fiber optic cable. Only 10% was ever lit. Most of the companies that built it went bankrupt. The infrastructure eventually became valuable — but not to the investors who paid for it.

The DeepSeek Shock. On January 27, 2025, a Chinese AI startup called DeepSeek released a ChatGPT-comparable model reportedly trained for just $5.6 million. NVIDIA lost $588.8 billion in market value in a single day — the largest single-day loss for any stock in human history. The company recovered, but the event exposed a vulnerability that the dot-com era never produced: the possibility that the technological moats justifying trillion-dollar valuations are shallower than investors believe. If an efficient challenger can produce comparable results at a fraction of the cost, the entire capex thesis is called into question.

Startup failures. Builder.ai, an AI-driven software development platform that raised hundreds of millions of dollars at a billion-dollar valuation, filed for bankruptcy after burning $445 million with no clear path to profitability. The story has become familiar. The AI startup ecosystem is producing the same pattern of rapid scaling, aggressive valuation, and sudden collapse that defined the dot-com era.

What Grantham Expects to Happen

Grantham is characteristically specific about how the unraveling would unfold. “Nvidia will lead it down and all the others will follow for a while,” he predicted. “And then out of the ashes, several of them will once again inherit the earth.”

In his Diary of a CEO interview in June 2026, he went further, calling AI “the biggest investment bubble in American history” and warning that leading AI stocks could fall by as much as 70%. He recommended that investors allocate roughly 60% of their portfolios to broad non-U.S. equity indices, alongside bonds, precious metals, and selected real estate.

The recommendation reflects a conviction that the damage will not be contained to AI-related stocks. If the valuations of the largest companies in the S&P 500 compress, the index itself will suffer. And if the capital that has flowed into AI infrastructure produces lower returns than expected, the spillover effects will be felt across the broader economy.

“Nvidia will lead it down and all the others will follow for a while. And then out of the ashes, several of them will once again inherit the earth.” — Jeremy Grantham, Bloomberg podcast, January 2026

The Counter-Argument

Counterpoint: For every bear argument, there is a rebuttal grounded in this era's fundamental differences from 1999. AI adoption is real, the leading companies are profitable, and the technology is delivering measurable productivity gains across industries. Corporate balance sheets are strong, and the spending cycle is being driven by genuine demand rather than speculative excess. “The difference between 1999 and today is that the internet was a story about potential,” said one technology fund manager. “AI is a story about something that is already happening. The valuations are high because the opportunity is real.” Even Grantham concedes that AI may be “the biggest idea of all time.” The question is not whether AI will transform the economy. It is whether the prices being paid for that transformation justify the returns investors expect.

The Verdict Not Yet In

Grantham’s track record demands respect, but it does not constitute certainty. He called the Japanese bubble correctly, but he also spent much of the 1990s warning about U.S. valuations before the market’s final, parabolic leg higher. Timing matters. A bubble can persist long enough to destroy the careers of those who bet against it.

What can be said with confidence is that the AI boom exhibits more of the classic features of a financial bubble than any market since 1999: extreme valuation dispersion, a compelling but untestable narrative, unprecedented capital flows, concentration risk, and early signs of capital destruction. The question that cannot yet be answered is whether the underlying strength of the leading companies will be sufficient to absorb a correction without it becoming a crash.

Grantham, for his part, has made his peace with the uncertainty. He has been called a permabear for so long that he wrote a memoir with precisely that title. But as he noted in his Fortune interview, he is not a bear. He is a dolphin — idealistic enough to pursue an uncomfortable truth, and experienced enough to know that being right early is not the same as being wrong.

“The probability that AI will not bust are slim to none,” he said. History suggests he is worth listening to.
